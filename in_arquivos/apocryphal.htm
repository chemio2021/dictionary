Apocryphal

PRONUNCIATION:
(uh-PAH-kri-fuhl) 
 
MEANING:
adjective:
1. Of dubious authorship or authenticity.
2. False; erroneous; fictitious.
 
ETYMOLOGY:
From Latin apocryphus (secret), from Greek apokruphos (secret, hidden), from apokruptein (to hide away), from apo- (away) + kruptein (to hide). Earliest documented use: 1590.
 
NOTES:
One may be a True Believer and might think the book of their religion -- no matter what religion they follow -- is the inerrant word of God, but they must first consider the question: Which version? Take the Bible, for instance. The Hebrew Bible / Old Testament is a collection of 40 to 50 books, depending on whom you ask. In Judaism, the Bible includes 41 books, in Christianity, many more. Even within Christianity, the Roman Catholic and Orthodox Churches include certain books in their versions of the Bible that Protestants do not.
In other words, some versions of Christianity accept certain books of the Bible as official or canonical while they reject the ones they consider as apocryphal or uncanonical. Let�s not even get into which translation.
