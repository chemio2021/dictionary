Bedizen (bi-DY-zuhn) verb tr.

 

   To dress or decorate in a showy or gaudy manner.

 

[From be- + dizen, from [possibly Low German] disen (to put flax on a distaff for spinning), from dis- (bunch of flax).]

 

Today's word and the word distaff share the same origin, dis- (a bunch of flax). A distaff is a staff with a cleft for holding wool, flax, etc. from which thread is drawn while being spun by hand. In olden times, spinning was considered a woman's work, so distaff figuratively referred to women. Distaff side (also spindle side) refers to the female side of a family. The

corresponding male equivalent of the term is spear side (also sword side).

Distaffs and spears are long gone -- what would be the modern stereotypical replacements of these terms?
